# *Overclassified* — Social Media Marketing Plan
**Vigilia Press | Dave Brown | Hilton Head Island, SC**
*Draft: June 2026*

---

## The Core Messaging Framework

Everything flows from one central idea: **the government has known, has documented it, and has chosen secrecy over accountability.** That is the book's thesis and it should be the spine of every social post, bio, and platform description.

Three messaging pillars branch from that spine:

**1. Credibility** — This is rigorously sourced. Not speculation, not fringe. Documents, hearings, named officials.

**2. Timeliness** — This is happening now. The June 9 Capitol event, the Turner document, disclosure pressure in Congress. The story is live.

**3. Stakes** — This isn't about aliens. It's about democratic accountability, classification abuse, and what the government owes the public.

Every post should touch at least one of these pillars. Rotating through all three keeps the feed from feeling repetitive or one-note.

## Platform Strategy

### X (Twitter) — Primary Platform. Non-Negotiable.

This is where UAP discourse lives. Researchers, journalists, congressional staffers, and the core readership are already there.

- Use your personal account, not a separate book account. Authenticity matters enormously in this space. A "brand" account feels corporate and cold.
- Follow and engage with: investigative journalists covering UAP (Ross Coulthart, Michael Shellenberger), the serious researcher accounts (not the fringe), and the congressional disclosure caucus orbit.
- **Post frequency:** 4–6 times per week minimum during launch window; 2–3 times per week in steady state.
- **Content mix:** one thread per week unpacking a primary source or document from the book; daily short-form takes on breaking UAP news tied back to your thesis; occasional behind-the-scenes on sourcing or writing process.

### LinkedIn — Secondary but Important

Reaches journalists, publishers, policy professionals, and the kind of serious adult reader who buys nonfiction trade books. Signals legitimacy to anyone who finds you through a press search.

- Personal profile, not a company page. Keep it as the author, not the publisher.
- **Post frequency:** 2–3 times per week.
- **Content tone:** More analytical, less reactive. Think op-ed excerpt, not hot take. "Here's what the Turner document actually says and why it matters" rather than "You won't believe this."
- Connect deliberately with journalists, think-tank people, and policy writers.

### Facebook — Existing Personal Presence

Don't open a separate business or author page yet. The algorithmic reach on new pages with no following is essentially zero. Your personal presence has existing reach — use it strategically.

- **Post frequency:** 2–3 times per week, mirroring LinkedIn content but slightly more conversational.
- Create a **private Facebook Group** for engaged readers and interested followers. This builds a community you control, outside the algorithm. Suggested name: *Overclassified — Readers & Discussion*.
- A public author page becomes worth building once you have enough momentum to seed it (post-launch, post any media coverage). At that point you can run paid promotion through it.

### Instagram — Existing Personal Presence

Instagram rewards visual content and is harder for a nonfiction author writing about documents and policy — but it's not a waste if approached correctly.

- Don't open a separate account. Convert your existing personal account to a **Creator account** (free, takes two minutes, gives you analytics and additional features without the coldness of a business page).
- **Content that works:** quote graphics pulled from the book or from declassified documents; archival images (government seals, congressional hearing screenshots, historical UAP-related imagery); simple document excerpts styled as graphics.
- **Post frequency:** 3–4 times per week, leaning on Reels and Stories for frequency — those don't require the same production investment as feed posts.
- Stories can be very casual: "Working on a source thread today," "This document from 1969 is wild," etc.

### Substack — Strong Consideration

Underrated for nonfiction authors and fits this material extremely well. A free newsletter on UAP disclosure, drawing on your research, positions you as the analytical voice in the space — not just an author selling a book.

- Launch before or simultaneous with the book.
- **Frequency:** Weekly or biweekly. Even one strong post per month builds a list you own.
- The list you build is yours — no algorithm, no platform risk.
- Paid tier is optional but can eventually generate revenue independent of book sales.

## Facebook/Instagram — Business vs. Personal Page

**Facebook:** Stay personal for now, add a Group. A business/author Page becomes useful when you have media coverage to funnel to it and a budget for paid ads. Creating one now with no audience just splits your energy.

**Instagram:** Creator account on your existing profile. It's the right middle ground — you get analytics and promotion tools without the cold-start problem of a new account with zero followers.

**Hard rule:** Don't start new accounts you can't feed consistently. One strong presence beats four neglected ones.

---

## Sync with Website and Book

The website (over-classified.com) should be the hub everything points to. Every platform bio links there. Every post that goes deep on a topic ends with "more at over-classified.com" or links to a specific page. The flow is:

**Social post → Website → Book purchase / email list signup**

The email list is more valuable than any social following. Prioritize capturing it. A simple lead magnet works well here — a free PDF excerpt, a one-page document summary, or a "key documents" guide drawn from your research.

**Content calendar logic:** Anchor each week around one substantive piece — a document thread on X, a LinkedIn post, a Substack entry — and then distribute shorter derivative versions across the other platforms. You're not creating four different things; you're creating one thing and adapting it.

## Social Media Management Tools

Rather than logging into each platform individually, a social media management tool lets you write a post once, select which platforms receive it, schedule the time, and have the software distribute it simultaneously. Most also aggregate replies, comments, and mentions into a single unified inbox. Analytics are consolidated into one dashboard.

**Key constraint:** Almost all tools require a Facebook Page or Instagram Business/Creator account — they cannot post to personal profiles due to Meta's API restrictions. This is the practical reason converting to a Creator account on Instagram and eventually creating a Facebook Page matters.

**Note on X/Twitter:** X tightened API access significantly in 2023. Verify current X support before committing to any tool.

### Tool Detail

**Buffer** ([buffer.com](https://buffer.com)) — Simplest and cleanest interface in the category. Best for someone who wants to get in, schedule posts, and get out. Strong free tier (3 platforms, 10 scheduled posts per platform). Paid plans start around $6/month per channel. Unified reply inbox on paid tier. Canva integration. Consistently rated best for Instagram reliability. **Recommended starting point.**

**Later** ([later.com](https://later.com)) — Strong on Instagram and TikTok. Good visual calendar and drag-and-drop planning. Free tier exists; paid starts around $18.75/month. Worth considering if Instagram becomes a priority channel, but weaker on X than Buffer.

**Publer** ([publer.com](https://publer.com)) — Less well known but strong value. Supports X, Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, Pinterest, YouTube, TikTok, Threads, Mastodon, Bluesky, and more. Bulk scheduling via CSV upload. Canva integration. Content recycling. Starts around $4/month per account (annual billing). X requires paid tier.

**Metricool** ([metricool.com](https://metricool.com)) — Best analytics in the value tier. Covers X, Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, TikTok, Pinterest, YouTube, and others. Unified inbox. Free tier available; Starter plan around $22/month for 1 brand (5 social accounts). Good pick if performance data matters more than simplicity.

**Hootsuite** ([hootsuite.com](https://hootsuite.com)) — The legacy market leader. More powerful but starts at ~$99/month. Overkill for a solo author operation. Not recommended at this scale.

---

### Practical Recommendation

**Buffer** at the paid tier (~$18–24/month for 3–4 platforms) is the right starting point. Low cost, low friction, does what you need. Add Metricool later if deeper analytics become a priority.

Workflow: write once in Buffer → select platforms → schedule → Buffer distributes → check the unified inbox once a day for replies.

Note that content still benefits from **light platform-specific adaptation**: X has character limits; LinkedIn rewards longer analytical posts; Instagram needs a visual attached. Most tools let you customize per platform within the same scheduling flow, so you're editing one post into slightly different versions rather than building from scratch four times.

# Addendum: Weekly Messaging Calendar

## Pre-Launch (Weeks 1–8 Before Launch)

The pre-launch period has one goal: build an audience before the book is available so launch week has something to launch *to*. Every post in this window should be establishing you as the credible analytical voice on UAP disclosure — not selling.

---

### Weeks 8–6 Before Launch — Establish Presence

**Theme: Who you are and why this matters**

- **X:** Introduce yourself and the project. Pin a thread: "I spent [X] years researching what the government actually knows about UAPs. Here's what the documents show." Three to five threads in this window, each unpacking one primary source from the book.
- **LinkedIn:** Two posts: one on your background and what compelled you to write the book; one analytical piece on the current disclosure landscape.
- **Instagram:** Graphics introducing the book. Quote from the introduction. Government document excerpt styled as a graphic.
- **Substack:** First post: "Why I wrote this book." Announce the newsletter, set expectations for what it covers.
- **Facebook (personal + Group):** Mirror the X intro thread in conversational form. Announce the Group.

**Messaging pillar this window: Credibility**

---

### Weeks 5–4 Before Launch — Build Anticipation

**Theme: The documents and what they reveal**

- **X:** Weekly document thread — one per week. Examples: the Turner document; the 1969 Bolender memo; the AATIP program origins; the June 9 Capitol event. Each thread ends with: "This is covered in depth in *Overclassified*, available [date]."
- **LinkedIn:** Two analytical posts. One on congressional disclosure dynamics; one on the classification system itself and why it matters for this issue.
- **Instagram:** Shift toward document graphics and countdown-style content. "X weeks until *Overclassified*." Quote graphics from the book.
- **Substack:** One post per week. Topics: a deep dive on a key document; a primer on how the classification system works; a summary of what the June 9 Capitol event means.
- **Facebook:** Shorter versions of the Substack posts. Group activity: pose discussion questions to build community engagement before launch.

**Messaging pillar this window: Timeliness — tie every post to something happening now**

---

### Weeks 3–2 Before Launch — Direct Promotion Begins

**Theme: The book is almost here**

- **X:** Shift 30% of posts to direct book promotion without dropping the analytical content. "Pre-order opens [date]." Share the cover. Share a key quote.
- **LinkedIn:** One post on the book's argument and what distinguishes it from other UAP coverage. One post announcing pre-order with a direct link.
- **Instagram:** Cover reveal if not already done. Pre-order graphics. Stories with countdown stickers.
- **Substack:** One post: "What's in the book and what you'll find that you won't find anywhere else."
- **Facebook:** Personal post announcing pre-order. Group post with more detail.
- **Email list:** First email to list: pre-order announcement with a brief excerpt.

**Messaging pillar this window: Stakes — why this book, why now**

---

### Week 1 Before Launch — Full Launch Push

**Theme: It's here**

- **X:** Daily posts. Mix of: final document threads; behind-the-scenes sourcing notes; direct book promotion; engagement with anyone discussing the book or the topic.
- **LinkedIn:** Three posts this week: launch announcement; a key excerpt or finding; a "what this book argues" summary.
- **Instagram:** Daily Stories. Feed posts every other day. Reels if feasible — even a simple talking-head video from you carries well.
- **Substack:** Launch week post: "The book is out. Here's what's in it."
- **Facebook:** Activate paid ads if a Page is live. Personal post on launch day. Group celebration post.
- **Email list:** Launch day email. Short, direct, with a purchase link.

---

## Launch Week

**Monday (Launch Day):**
- X: Pin a new thread — "The book is out. Here's the argument in five points."
- LinkedIn: Formal launch announcement.
- Instagram: Launch day graphic, Stories all day.
- Substack: Send the launch email.
- Facebook: Personal post + Group post + paid ad if ready.

**Tuesday–Thursday:**
- X: Engage with everyone who mentions the book or the topic. Post a daily insight or document excerpt.
- LinkedIn: Respond to comments. Post one substantive engagement piece.
- Instagram: Stories daily. Behind-the-scenes or reader responses if any come in.

**Friday:**
- X: First-week recap thread. "Here's what the response has looked like and what people are saying."
- LinkedIn: A reflective post — what writing the book taught you.
- Substack: Short "launch week notes" post for subscribers.

## Post-Launch (Weeks 1–8 After Launch)

The post-launch period sustains momentum and converts ongoing UAP news into continued book visibility. The goal shifts from announcement to establishing long-term authority.

---

### Weeks 1–4 Post-Launch — Sustain Momentum

**Theme: The story keeps developing**

- **X:** Return to weekly document threads. Tie breaking UAP news to the book's argument. Engage actively with the UAP discourse community. Repost reviews and reader responses.
- **LinkedIn:** Two posts per week. Reviews, reader responses, and analytical takes on new developments.
- **Instagram:** Reader response graphics. Pull quotes from favorable reviews. Reels with brief "what the book covers" walkthroughs.
- **Substack:** Weekly post. Tie new developments to book content. This is where your deepest readers live — reward them with material that goes beyond what's in the book.
- **Facebook:** Group discussion threads. Prompt readers: "What chapter surprised you most?" "What document from the book do you want to know more about?"

**Messaging pillar this window: Credibility + Timeliness combined**

---

### Weeks 5–8 Post-Launch — Establish Long-Term Authority

**Theme: You are the source**

- Frequency can drop slightly but quality must hold. 3–4 X posts per week; 1–2 LinkedIn; Substack biweekly minimum.
- Begin pitching op-eds to outlets covering national security, transparency, or UAP — and use your social presence as proof of audience.
- Seek podcast appearances. The UAP podcast ecosystem is large and actively looks for credible guests. Use X to connect with hosts.
- Any media coverage (interviews, reviews, mentions) gets amplified across all platforms immediately.
- Evaluate whether paid ads should continue, based on sales data.

**Messaging pillar this window: Stakes — the long-term democratic accountability argument**

## Evergreen Content to Keep in Rotation

These can be scheduled in advance and recycled:

- Weekly document thread (always fresh — there's no shortage of material)
- "What the classification system actually does" explainer
- Reader questions answered
- "This happened in [year] and nobody noticed" historical callbacks
- Responses to breaking UAP news tied back to book arguments
- Pull quotes from the book — one per week indefinitely
